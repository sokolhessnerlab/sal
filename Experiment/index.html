Debrief

Thank you for taking part in this study. The purpose of the task is to understand how the things that people learn about someone - and the order in which we learn those things - influences our desire to connect. We also wanted to see whether the things we learn about someone result in differences in the types of connections we wish to have with them. To measure social connection, we drew from current theories in the field of social psychology that define social connection along two dimensions - affiliation (intimacy, trustworthiness) and power (dominance, hierarchy). In this task, we are also testing whether there is evidence for a possible third dimension, value, which measures the extent to which one benefits from a particular social connection.

During this task, we had you learn about 12 characters, each of whom had names and faces randomly matched up with a series of vignettes that fell along two dimensions: valence (whether the vignette was positive or negative) and attribution (whether the behavior described in the vignette was caused by the situation or by the character's disposition). For some characters, the dimensions of the vignettes did not change (for example, all 10 vignettes were positive and situational). In other characters, one or both of the dimensions changed after the first 5 vignettes. By keeping some characters consistent and having others shift, we can look at how desire to connect with a person changes when the things we learn about them change.

In addition to asking how your desire to connect changes on a vignette by vignette basis, we also asked general questions about your feelings toward each character to get a sense for how all of the information learned informs a general conception of the character, as well as the specific types of connections you may be interested in. In order to obtain a baseline understanding for how each participant's individual desires for social connection may factor into their feelings and decisions, we also asked general questions about how much you want to connect with others and the types of connections you tend to value.

Overall, our goal with this study is to deepen our understanding of how we form social connections and the different types of connections we can choose to have with others. Your participation in this task helps us gather information about how people may use social information in choosing with whom to affiliate.
